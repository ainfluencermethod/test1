Static images got you here. Video gets you to the next level.

Every major platform has made the same bet: video-first algorithms. Instagram gives Reels 3x the organic reach of static posts. TikTok is video. YouTube Shorts crossed 70 billion daily views. The Toolkit

Four tools. That's all you need. Each one has a specific job, and knowing when to reach for which one is half the battle.

Kling 3.0 — Your Primary Weapon

KlingAI's flagship model. Best-in-class for image-to-video with character consistency. The Pro tier gives you 10-second clips at 1080p with motion control, and the quality is genuinely impressive.

  • Best for: Character animation, lifestyle content, product shots
  • Image-to-video: Upload your AI character image → describe motion → generate
  • Text-to-video: Works but less consistent for established characters
  • Motion Control: Camera paths, zoom, pan, orbit — all adjustable
  • Cost: ~$0.30 per generation (Pro plan), 66 credits/month at $8/mo

SeeDance 2.0 — Dance & Performance

The specialist. Feed it a reference dance video and your AI character image, and it maps the motion onto your character. The results are shockingly good for trend recreation.

  • Best for: Dance trends, performance videos, choreographed content
  • Input: Reference video + character image → motion-mapped output
  • Limitation: Works best with full-body shots and clear backgrounds
  • Cost: Free via Replicate (~$0.05/gen) or run locally with a 24GB GPU

Wan 2.1 / 2.2 — Open Source Power

Alibaba's open-source video generation model. Run it locally for zero per-generation cost, or use it through Replicate and Hugging Face for cheap API access. Version 2.2 added significant quality improvements.

  • Best for: Batch generation, experimentation, budget-conscious creators

Google DeepMind's latest. Exceptional at cinematic quality and natural physics. Access through Google AI Studio or the Vertex AI API. The built-in audio generation is a unique advantage.

  • Best for: Cinematic content, natural movement, audio-inclusive clips

  • Access: Google AI Studio (free tier) or Vertex AI API
  • Limitation: Less control over specific character consistency
  • Cost: Free tier available, Pro at ~$0.20/gen
The Smart Stack

Kling 3.0 for your hero content (the posts that matter). SeeDance 2.0 for dance trends and performance clips. Wan 2.2 for batch generation and experimentation. Veo 3.1 for cinematic one-offs where audio matters. Don't try to use one tool for everything.

Module 02
Image-to-Video
Workflow

This is the core skill. You already have great AI character images. Now you make them move.

The workflow is deceptively simple: upload your image → write a motion prompt → generate → review → refine. The magic is in the motion prompt — how you describe the movement determines everything.

Step 1: Prepare Your Source Image

Not every image works well for animation. The best source images for video generation share specific traits:

  • Clean composition — character clearly visible, not cropped awkwardly
  • Neutral or slight expression — extreme expressions limit animation possibilities
  • Proper aspect ratio — 9:16 for Reels/TikTok, 16:9 for YouTube, 1:1 for feed
  • High resolution — minimum 1024×1024, ideally higher
  • Good lighting — even, flattering light gives the model more to work with
Pro Tip

Generate your source image specifically for video. In Nano Banana Pro, add "poised, neutral expression, slight head tilt, even studio lighting" to your prompt. This gives Kling maximum flexibility for the animation.

Step 2: Kling 3.0 Pro Settings

Open KlingAI → Select "Image to Video" → Upload your source image. Then configure:

  • Model: Kling 3.0 (not 2.x — the difference is significant)
  • Mode: Pro (Standard works but Pro is noticeably better)
  • Duration: 5s for first tests, 10s for final content
  • Aspect Ratio: Match your source image
  • Motion Intensity: Start at 5/10, adjust based on content type
  • Negative Prompt: "distortion, morphing, blurry, jittery, glitch"

Module 02
Motion Prompting

The motion prompt is everything. A bad prompt wastes your credits. A great prompt creates content that stops scrolls. Here's the framework:

The Motion Prompt Formula

[Subject Action] + [Body Details] + [Expression/Mood] + [Camera Movement] + [Environment Detail]

Example: "The woman slowly turns her head to the right, her hair gently swaying. She gives a soft, confident smile. Subtle camera push-in. Warm golden hour light."

5 Example Prompts (with Explanations)

Prompt 01 — The Slow Turn
"The woman slowly turns her head from left to right, her eyes meeting the camera with a confident gaze. Hair moves naturally with the motion. Slight smile forms on her lips. Cinematic, 24fps feel."

Why it works: Simple, clear motion. One primary action. Specific details about expression and hair physics.

Prompt 02 — Walking Toward Camera
"The woman walks confidently toward the camera on a city street, maintaining eye contact. Her outfit moves naturally with each step. Shallow depth of field, background softly blurred. Smooth tracking shot."

Why it works: Combines character motion with camera style. Depth of field instruction creates cinematic quality.

Prompt 03 — The Hair Flip
"The woman tosses her hair back with a quick, playful motion, then looks directly at the camera with a bold expression. Wind catches the hair mid-flip. Bright, high-contrast lighting."

Why it works: Dynamic action with specific physics. The wind detail helps the model create realistic hair movement.

Prompt 04 — Typing / Working
"The woman sits at a sleek desk, typing on a laptop. She occasionally glances up at the camera with a focused expression. Soft ambient lighting, modern interior. Camera slowly orbits from side to front."

Why it works: Great for "day in the life" content. The orbit camera adds production value without complex motion.

Prompt 05 — Reaction / Surprise
"The woman's eyes widen slightly as she reads something on her phone, then she breaks into a genuine laugh, covering her mouth briefly. Natural, spontaneous feeling. Close-up framing."

Why it works: Emotional range is impressive with Kling 3.0. The specificity of the micro-expressions sells it.

Stitching Multiple Clips

For longer content, generate multiple 10-second clips and stitch them in CapCut. The trick: use the last frame of clip A as the source image for clip B. This maintains visual continuity between segments. Screenshot the final frame, use it as input for the next generation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overcomplicating the prompt — one clear action per clip beats three conflicting ones
  • Ignoring aspect ratio — mismatched ratios crop your character awkwardly
  • Setting motion intensity too high — leads to distortion and warping artifacts
  • Skipping the negative prompt — always include "distortion, morphing, blurry" at minimum
  • Using low-res source images — garbage in, garbage out applies doubly for video

Module 03
Motion Control

Motion control is what separates amateur AI video from cinematic AI video. It's the difference between a static image that slightly moves and a clip that feels like it was shot by a professional camera operator.

Kling 3.0's Motion Control feature lets you define camera paths independently from character movement. This is powerful — and most people don't use it.

The Six Camera Movements

Pan

Camera moves horizontally. Left-to-right reveals are great for "room tour" and "outfit check" content. Use sparingly — it can feel jarring on short clips.

Tilt

Camera moves vertically. Tilt-up from feet to face is classic for "outfit reveal" videos. Tilt-down works for dramatic reveals.

Zoom

Push in for intimacy, pull out for context. Slow zoom-in on face during emotional content. Zoom-out to reveal environment or surprise element.

Orbit

Camera circles the subject. The most cinematic option. Even a quarter-orbit (90°) adds massive production value. Best for beauty and fashion content.

Track

Camera follows the subject's movement. Essential for walking shots. Keeps the character centered while the background moves naturally.

Static

No camera movement. Underrated. Lets the character's motion speak for itself. Best for reaction content and talking-head style clips.

Module 03
Combining Character + Camera Motion

The real magic happens when you layer character animation with camera movement. Here are proven combinations:

01
Walk + Track (Follow Shot)
Character walks forward. Camera tracks alongside or follows behind. Classic influencer "walking through the city" content. Set motion intensity to 6/10.
02
Head Turn + Slow Zoom (The Reveal)
Character turns head toward camera as camera slowly pushes in. Perfect for "POV: someone calls your name" content. Extremely engaging hook.
03
Pose + Orbit (Beauty Shot)
Character holds a pose or makes subtle movements while camera orbits 90°. Fashion and beauty content staple. Set orbit speed to slow — fast orbits look cheap.
04
Gesture + Static (The Reaction)
Character reacts with expression or gesture. No camera movement. Lets the performance carry the clip. Best for meme-style content and reactions.
05
Full Body + Tilt Up (The Reveal)
Camera starts at feet, tilts up slowly to reveal full outfit/character. The "Get Ready With Me" payoff shot. Set tilt speed to match music beat if using audio.
Common Mistake

Don't combine complex character motion with complex camera motion. If the character is doing a lot (dancing, walking, gesturing), keep the camera simple (static or slow zoom). If the character is still, that's when you go cinematic with the camera. One element leads, the other supports.

Troubleshooting Artifacts

  • Warping / morphing face: Reduce motion intensity to 3–4. Add "maintain facial consistency, no morphing" to prompt. Use negative prompt: "face distortion, warping."
  • Jittery movement: This usually means conflicting motion instructions. Simplify your prompt to one primary action.
  • Character drifting off-frame: Add "character stays centered in frame" to prompt. Reduce motion intensity.
  • Unnatural hand movement: Hands are still the hardest part. Avoid prompts that require detailed hand actions. Keep hands in pockets, on hips, or out of frame when possible.
  • Background warping: Add "stable background, no distortion" to prompt. Use source images with simpler backgrounds.

Module 04
Dance & Performance
Videos

Dance trends are the fastest path to viral content on TikTok and Reels. The problem was always getting an AI character to dance convincingly. SeeDance 2.0 solves this.

The concept is simple: you provide a reference video of someone performing a dance or action, plus your AI character image, and SeeDance maps the motion from the reference onto your character. The result is your AI influencer performing that exact choreography.

SeeDance 2.0 Workflow

01
Find Your Reference Video
Browse TikTok or Instagram for trending dances. Download the reference clip — you need the actual video file. Use SaveTT.cc or similar to download. The cleaner the reference (clear body visibility, simple background), the better the output.
02
Prepare Your Character Image
Use a full-body shot of your AI character. Standing pose works best. Clean background (solid color or simple environment). Resolution should be at least 768×1024. The pose should roughly match the starting pose of the reference video.
03
Run SeeDance 2.0
Upload both files to SeeDance (via Replicate API or Hugging Face Space). Set output resolution to match your target platform. Processing takes 2–5 minutes depending on clip length. Generate 2–3 variations and pick the best.
04
Review and Clean Up
Check for artifacts, especially around hands and feet. Minor glitches can be hidden with CapCut transitions, text overlays, or strategic cropping. If the output is too glitchy, try a different source image pose.

Module 04
Lip Sync & Audio Matching

Lip sync adds another layer of realism to your AI content. While perfect sync is still evolving, there are reliable approaches that work today.

Method 1: Audio-Driven Lip Sync

Use tools like Hedra or Sync Labs to add lip movement to your AI video. Upload your generated video + an audio file, and the tool maps mouth movements to match the speech.

  • Best for: Voiceover content, "speaking to camera" clips
  • Quality: 7/10 — good enough for most content, not perfect
  • Cost: Hedra starts at $8/mo, Sync Labs has pay-per-use

Method 2: Music Video Approach

Skip lip sync entirely. Use your AI video clips as a music video — overlay trending audio, let the movement match the vibe rather than the lyrics. This is actually how most viral AI content works.

  • Best for: Dance content, aesthetic/mood videos, trend recreation
  • Quality: 10/10 — no sync issues because there's nothing to sync
  • Cost: Free (just add music in CapCut)

Creating "Reaction" and "POV" Content

These formats are massive on Reels and TikTok, and they're perfect for AI video because they require minimal complex motion:

  • POV: Someone compliments you — Generate a subtle smile reaction. Static camera. 3–5 seconds. Add text overlay with the scenario.
  • POV: You see your ex — Hair flip + confident walk. Track camera. Layer trending audio.
  • When he says [X] — Expression change from neutral to [reaction]. Static camera. Close-up framing. The simpler the motion, the more believable.
The 80/20 Rule

80% of viral AI video content uses simple motions: head turns, smiles, hair flips, walking. Don't overcomplicate it. Simple motion + great audio + good text overlay = viral formula.

Module 04
Best Practices for Performance Content
Finding the Right Reference Videos

Not all reference videos work equally well with SeeDance 2.0. Here's what to look for:

✓ Good Reference Videos

  • Full body visible throughout the entire clip
  • Simple, uncluttered background (solid wall, studio)
  • Consistent lighting — no dramatic shadows or flashing
  • Single person (multi-person confuses the motion extraction)
  • Smooth, deliberate movements (not erratic or extremely fast)
  • 15 seconds or less (shorter = higher quality output)

✗ Bad Reference Videos

  • Frequent camera cuts or angle changes
  • Person partially obscured or going off-frame
  • Multiple people dancing (confuses pose detection)
  • Very fast, complex choreography (quality drops sharply)
  • Low resolution or heavy motion blur

Scaling Dance Content

Once you find a workflow that produces good results, scale it:

  1. Save a library of 20–30 proven reference dances — clips you know work well with SeeDance
  2. Generate 3 character outfits — same character, different looks, using your image generation pipeline
  3. Batch generate — each reference × each outfit = dozens of unique clips from minimal effort
  4. Schedule releases — one dance video per day keeps the algorithm engaged

This batch approach turns dance content from a creative grind into a repeatable system.

Module 05
Post-Production

Raw AI-generated video is your starting point, not your final product. Post-production is where you turn a good clip into a scroll-stopping piece of content. The difference between 1K views and 100K views is often in the edit.

CapCut Editing Workflow

CapCut is the standard for short-form video editing. It's free, fast, and built for Reels/TikTok content. Here's the workflow:

01
Import & Trim
Import your AI-generated clip. Trim the first and last 0.5 seconds — AI video models often have slight artifacts at the start/end. Cut to the cleanest portion.
02
Add Music / Audio
Choose trending audio from CapCut's library or import your own. Match the energy of the visual to the audio. Time cuts and key moments to beat drops.
03
Text Overlays
Add hook text in the first 1–2 seconds. Use CapCut's built-in text styles or create consistent branded text. Font: bold sans-serif, white with subtle shadow.
04
Color Grade
AI video often looks slightly flat. Add a subtle contrast boost (+10–15%), slight warmth (+5 temperature), and a touch of saturation (+10%). Don't overdo it.
05
Export
Export at 1080p, 30fps (or 60fps for dance content). H.265 codec if available. Maximum quality settings. File size shouldn't exceed 50MB for most platforms.

Upscaling & Quality Enhancement

AI-generated video at native resolution can look soft or slightly compressed. Upscaling fixes this and adds a professional polish that separates your content from the crowd.

Topaz Video AI

The gold standard for video upscaling. Takes your 1080p AI video and enhances it to crisp, detailed 4K. The "Iris" model is specifically good for face enhancement.

  • Best settings: Iris model, 2x upscale, De-noise at 15, Sharpen at 20
  • Processing time: ~3–5 minutes per 10-second clip on a decent GPU
  • Cost: $199 one-time purchase (worth it if you're serious)

Free Alternative: Real-ESRGAN (Video)

Open-source upscaler that runs locally. Not as refined as Topaz but significantly better than raw output.

  • Best settings: RealESRGAN_x4plus model, 2x scale
  • Processing time: Slower than Topaz, ~8–10 minutes per clip
  • Cost: Free

Module 05
Batch Processing for Scale

Once your workflow is dialed in, the goal is volume. Here's how to turn a 1-video-per-day habit into a 5-video-per-day machine:

The Batch Pipeline

  1. Monday: Generate source images. Create 10–15 character images in different poses, outfits, and settings using your image pipeline.
  2. Tuesday: Generate videos. Run all images through Kling 3.0 and SeeDance 2.0. Queue them up and let them process. Aim for 20+ raw clips.
  3. Wednesday: Upscale. Batch-process all videos through Topaz Video AI. Queue overnight if needed.
  4. Thursday: Edit. Open CapCut, assembly-line your edits. Trim, add audio, text overlays, color grade. Export all.
  5. Friday: Schedule. Upload everything to your scheduling tool. Set posts for the next 1–2 weeks.
Assembly Line Mindset

Don't create one video from start to finish, then start the next. Do all your image generation at once. All your video generation at once. All your editing at once. Batching the same task is 3–4x faster than switching between different tasks constantly. Your brain (and your GPU) work better in focused mode.

Recommended Posting Cadence

  • Instagram Reels: 1–2 per day (more gets throttled)
  • TikTok: 2–3 per day (algorithm rewards volume)
  • YouTube Shorts: 1 per day (consistency > volume)
  • Twitter/X: 1–2 per day (engagement timing matters more)

At 5 unique videos per batch day, one batch session produces a full week of content across all platforms.

The Loop Strategy

The algorithm measures "completion rate" — what percentage of viewers watch to the end. Short videos get watched multiple times (looped), which counts as multiple completions. A 7-second video watched 3 times beats a 30-second video watched once. When in doubt, shorter wins.

The Hook Formula

Every successful short-form video follows this structure:

  1. Hook (0–1.5 seconds): Visual or text that stops the scroll. This is non-negotiable. If they don't stop, nothing else matters.
  2. Payoff (1.5–8 seconds): The content they stopped for. Make it satisfying. Match the hook's promise.
  3. CTA (last 2 seconds): Give them something to do — comment, share, follow, check bio. Even a simple "👀" text overlay with a question works.

Nail the hook. The rest is execution.
