Chapter 1
What is a DNA file?

A DNA file is the single source of truth for your AI character. It defines what never changes, what can vary, and how every new image should still feel like the same person.

Think of it like a creative operating system: face geometry, age band, body proportions, style signature, camera defaults, lighting presets, recurring environments, emotional range, and prompt syntax.

Pro Tip

Write your DNA file before you chase content ideas. Concept without identity creates short-term spikes and long-term slop.

Consistency = follows because recognition lowers friction.

When viewers can recognize your model in half a second, they don’t need to re-decide whether they care. That alone increases retention and profile taps.

A weak DNA file sounds like

“Brunette girl, pretty face, stylish, realistic.” That gives the model too much room to improvise.

A strong DNA file sounds like

“24–27, almond hazel eyes, soft square jaw, narrow nose bridge, medium olive skin with visible cheek texture, athletic hourglass frame, silver hoop signature, natural-lux styling.”

  • Define what must stay fixed in every generation.
  • Separate fixed traits from variable scene details.
  • Store one approved reference image and one written DNA file together.
DNA CORE = fixed identity + repeatable style + repeatable camera + repeatable mood + prompt structure
Your Turn

Write one sentence that explains who your character is, what lane she plays in, and what visual memory you want her to create instantly.

Chapter 2
Face architecture

Most inconsistency starts in the face. If the generator is free to reinterpret jawline, eye spacing, age, skin texture, or nose shape every time, you are not building a character. You are rolling dice.

01

Lock the facial skeleton

Write down bone structure first: jaw shape, cheekbone prominence, forehead width, chin shape, nose bridge, lip volume, eye shape, brow density, and symmetry level.

02

Lock the age impression

Use a 3-year range, not a single magical number. “24–27” keeps the vibe stable better than “25 exactly.”

03

Lock the texture cues

Texture is identity. Freckles, matte skin, dewy finish, under-eye softness, lip texture, baby hairs, subtle pores — these are memory hooks.

Pro Tip

Choose 3 “can’t-miss” facial signatures. Example: cat-eye tilt, soft square jaw, full lower lip. If those survive every prompt, the face survives.

  • Write 8–12 face descriptors, not 3 vague adjectives.
  • Choose one hero reference image and one backup reference only.
  • Reject outputs that are beautiful but off-model. Pretty is not the same as on-brand.
FACE ANCHOR: medium olive skin, almond hazel eyes, softly arched dark brows, narrow nose bridge, defined cupid's bow, soft square jaw, visible cheek texture, 24–27
Your Turn

List your character’s 3 non-negotiable facial signatures, then write a one-line face anchor prompt using only those traits plus skin tone and age range.

Chapter 3
Body consistency

Body drift is the silent killer. One image looks petite, the next looks statuesque, the next suddenly reads bodybuilder. Viewers notice even when they cannot articulate it.

Define body type in terms of proportion, not only weight words. Use shoulder width, waist contrast, leg length impression, bust balance, glute emphasis, muscle visibility, and posture.

Pro Tip

Use “height impression” instead of exact height when prompting visuals. “Long-legged, medium-tall impression” often stays more stable than writing 5'8.

What to specify

  • Body type: slim, athletic, curvy-athletic, petite, etc.
  • Proportion ratio: shoulder-to-waist-to-hip read.
  • Fitness level: soft, toned, visibly athletic, highly defined.
  • Posture defaults: relaxed, upright, feminine, dominant.
  • Leg and torso balance: short torso / long legs, balanced, etc.

Across 100+ images

Reuse the same body paragraph in every prompt. Change wardrobe, scene, and expression — not the proportions.

Reference tactic

Create a body sheet with 4 angles: front standing, 3/4 standing, seated, and walking. Approve one of each before scaling production.

BODY ANCHOR: athletic hourglass proportions, medium-tall impression, long legs, defined waist, toned shoulders, soft abdominal definition, confident upright posture
Your Turn

Write your body anchor in one sentence. Then define one pose the character should look good in every single time.

Chapter 4
Style & wardrobe

Wardrobe is where brand recognition accelerates. A signature necklace, recurring cut of top, repeat jacket silhouette, or fixed palette can do more for memorability than ten extra adjectives in your prompt.

Build a style stack

Start with three layers: everyday uniform, “elevated” uniform, and content-specific outfit variations. Each should still feel like the same character, not a costume change into someone else.

Pick a palette

Choose 2 neutrals + 1 accent habit. Example: black, charcoal, washed denim, plus silver hardware. This keeps outputs legible and premium.

Pro Tip

Scroll-speed recognition comes from recurring silhouettes: cropped zip hoodie, oversized blazer, ribbed tank, leather jacket, oversized white shirt. People remember shapes faster than details.

  • Define 3 signature outfits with exact descriptors.
  • Choose 2 recurring accessories max.
  • Ban wardrobe pieces that clash with the niche.
  • Keep hair styling compatible with the wardrobe identity.
STYLE ANCHOR: clean luxury streetwear, fitted ribbed tanks, cropped jackets, charcoal-black-silver palette, subtle silver hoops, minimal glossy manicure
Your Turn

Write your character’s default everyday fit, best-performing “hero” fit, and one accessory that should appear in at least 60% of posts.

Chapter 5
Camera language
Your character should not only look the same. She should feel like she is always shot by the same photographer.

Camera language is the invisible glue that turns random scenes into a coherent feed.

Define a small set of default framing rules: portrait close-up, waist-up 3/4, full-body with negative space, mirror selfie, low-angle walking shot, etc. Then define your lens behavior: 35mm lifestyle, 50mm portrait, 85mm compressed beauty, or phone-camera realism.

Pro Tip

One account = one camera religion. If half your feed looks like editorial studio and half looks like random Android snapshots, coherence dies.

Default shots

  • 40% close portrait
  • 30% waist-up
  • 20% full body
  • 10% experimental

Lens rules

  • Beauty content: 85mm compressed portrait
  • Lifestyle: 35mm natural scene
  • Social-native realism: phone camera, slight grain
CAMERA RULES: eye-level or slightly low angle, 50mm natural portrait look, centered subject with negative space, shallow depth of field, clean subject separation
Your Turn

Create your shot ratio. Decide which 3 framings will make up 80% of your feed and which lens look will own the account.

Chapter 6
Lighting presets

Lighting is emotional identity. Keep 3–4 repeatable presets and your character will hold the same mood even when the settings change.

Preset 1 — Golden hour

Warm, directional side light. Soft skin glow. Best for aspirational lifestyle, soft glamour, travel, and romantic reels.

Preset 2 — Clean studio

Bright, controlled, frontal or butterfly-style light. Best for hero beauty shots, brand collabs, product content, and polished thumbnails.

Preset 3 — Moody / noir

Low-key directional light, deeper shadows, cinematic contrast. Best for teaser content, mystery, nightlife, or alt niches.

Preset 4 — Natural window

Soft daylight, gentle falloff, believable home or café realism. Best for relatable, girl-next-door, and intimate content.

Pro Tip

Do not invent lighting from scratch every time. Name your presets in the DNA file, then insert them like modules inside prompts.

  • Pick one default lighting preset used in 50%+ of posts.
  • Assign each preset to a content category.
  • Keep skin rendering similar across all presets.
LIGHTING PRESET: soft golden-hour side light, warm highlights, gentle cheekbone contour, controlled shadow depth, natural skin texture preserved
Your Turn

Name your 4 lighting presets and decide what type of post each preset is allowed to serve.

AI Influencer Blueprint · 8
Chapter 7
Environment rules

Characters feel real when they appear to exist in a world, not a void. Recurring environments create continuity and implied lifestyle.

Define 4–6 “home base” environments: apartment interior, luxury bathroom, gym mirror, café patio, sunset balcony, black SUV passenger seat, etc. Then define the ratio between indoor and outdoor content.

Pro Tip

Pick locations your niche would logically revisit. The world should feel lived in, not like a stock photo scavenger hunt.

Environment filters

  • Does this setting match the character’s status and vibe?
  • Does it repeat often enough to become familiar?
  • Does the background distract from the subject?
  • Can you describe it in one clean sentence?

Indoor / outdoor ratio

Example: 70% indoor controlled aesthetic, 30% outdoor cinematic lifestyle. Ratios stop your feed from becoming visually random.

Background style

Minimal and premium beats cluttered and “busy.” Walls, glass, concrete, wood, muted greenery, clean bedding, brushed metal — keep it legible.

ENVIRONMENT RULE: modern neutral apartment interiors, luxury gym mirrors, rooftop sunset moments, upscale café patios; 70% indoor / 30% outdoor
Your Turn

Choose 5 recurring locations and write one sentence each describing what makes them feel like your character’s world.

Chapter 8
Expression library

Expression is behavioral branding. The right face for the right content type makes posts feel intentional. Build 8–12 signature expressions and map each one to a use case.

Soft smile

Lifestyle, wellness, “good morning” content.

Half smirk

Teasers, flirtation, high-curiosity posts.

Neutral gaze

Beauty shots, product focus, authority.

Laughing candid

Relatability, behind-the-scenes energy.

Bedroom-soft eyes

Intimacy, premium subscription conversion.

Raised brow

Hooks, reactions, meme-led content.

Pro Tip

Do not write “seductive expression” and pray. Use physically observable cues: parted lips, softened eyelids, asymmetrical smile, direct eye contact, chin tilt.

  • Name every expression in plain language.
  • Map each expression to a content category.
  • Keep mouth shape and eye energy consistent with the niche.
EXPRESSION CUE: direct eye contact, subtle half-smirk, relaxed brows, slightly parted lips, confident but warm energy

Create your top 8 expressions and assign each one to a specific post type: lifestyle, gym, teaser, promo, reply bait, story content, etc.

Chapter 10
Prompt engineering for consistency

Prompts work best when they are modular. Stop writing one giant paragraph from scratch every time. Build stacked prompt blocks in a fixed order.

01

Identity block

Name, age impression, face anchor, body anchor, hair, skin, signature traits.

02

Style block

Wardrobe, palette, accessory rules, grooming details.

03

Scene block

Environment, action, pose, mood, lighting preset.

04

Camera block

Framing, angle, lens, depth of field, realism cue.

05

Quality / exclusion block

Photorealism notes, texture notes, avoiders, anatomy cleanup.

Pro Tip

Keep the first two blocks almost identical every time. Most variation should happen in the scene block, not the identity block.

[IDENTITY] + [STYLE] + [EXPRESSION] + [SCENE] + [LIGHTING] + [CAMERA] + [QUALITY] + [EXCLUSIONS]
Your Turn

Build your reusable prompt skeleton with labeled blocks. Then save it as a template instead of rewriting from memory each session.

Chapter 11
Testing your DNA

Do not call the DNA file finished because one hero image slapped. A real DNA system survives variation. The test is simple: generate 10 images across controlled changes and score them hard.

Test set

  • 2 close-up portraits
  • 2 waist-up lifestyle shots
  • 2 full-body shots
  • 2 different lighting presets
  • 2 different environments

Scorecard

  • Face match /10
  • Body match /10
  • Style match /10
  • Camera consistency /10
  • Mood consistency /10
Pro Tip

If one category repeatedly fails, do not “hope it gets better.” Strengthen that exact section of the DNA file and retest.

  • Average score should be 8/10 or higher across all categories.
  • If face match falls below 7, rebuild the face anchor.
  • If body drifts, reduce pose complexity and tighten body wording.
  • If style drifts, simplify wardrobe vocabulary and accessories.
CONSISTENCY SCORE = (face + body + style + camera + mood) / 5
Your Turn

Run a 10-image test batch, score every image, and write a one-line diagnosis for the lowest-scoring category before editing anything else.

AI Influencer Blueprint · 15
Chapter 12
Common mistakes

1. Overwriting identity every prompt

Fix: lock your identity block and reuse it almost unchanged.

2. Using vague beauty language

Fix: replace “gorgeous, stunning, perfect” with observable traits.

3. Too many style personas

Fix: pick one visual lane and stay inside it for at least 30 posts.

4. Inconsistent camera logic

Fix: define 3 default framings and one dominant lens feel.

5. Falling in love with off-model images

Fix: beauty is irrelevant if it breaks recognition. Kill it and move on.

6. Testing too little

Fix: every serious character needs a batch test, scorecard, and revision cycle.

Pro Tip

The worst habit is letting “pretty enough” replace “strategic enough.” Slop can get likes. It rarely builds an empire.

RULE: if an output cannot be identified as your character without the username attached, it probably does not belong in the feed
Your Turn

Review your last 20 generated images and label each one: on-model, borderline, or off-model. Delete or archive the off-model set.
